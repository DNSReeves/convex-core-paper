Convex Core, from the word up

What “convex” means, how it lives inside w_equity + w_convexity + w_duration = 1, and why these specific ETFs.

1 · Start with the word: convex

Convex just means curved — and in investing it means a payoff that curves in your favor: you give up only a little when you’re wrong, and the protection grows fastest exactly when you need it most. The opposite of a straight line.

“Own the market” (buy-and-hold) is a straight line: if the market falls 37%, you fall 37%. Convex Core deliberately bends that line so a 37% market crash costs you a fraction of that — while you keep most of the upside. That bend is the whole idea.

Figure 1. The straight grey line is buy-and-hold. The red curve is Convex Core. On the downside (left) the curve lifts away from the line — that gold gap is the convexity: crash protection that grows as the crash deepens. On the upside it gives up a little. (Illustrative shape; the 2008 point −37%→≈−1% is the real backtest.)
The intuition in one line: a straight line treats a −37% crash and a −5% dip the same way (proportionally). A convex payoff fights hardest in the deep left tail — exactly where buy-and-hold hurts most.

Why the tail is worth obsessing over

Losses are asymmetric: the gain you need to recover grows much faster than the loss. Lose 20% and you need +25% back. Lose 50% and you need +100%. Avoiding the deep holes matters more for long-run wealth than catching every last rally.

Figure 2. Recovery math is convex too. A buy-and-hold investor down 55% (SPY, 2008–09) needs +122% just to break even; Convex Core’s worst drawdown of ~−18% needs only +22%.

2 · How that bend lives in the equation

w_equity + w_convexity + w_duration = 1

This is a budget. Every dollar is fully invested (the weights sum to 1 — Convex Core is long-only, no leverage), and it splits across three jobs:

TermIts jobShape
w_equity Participate. Own the market so you compound with it (~73% of years are up). Linear — this is the straight line.
w_convexity Profit from chaos. Trend-following that goes short falling assets — it pays in sustained crashes. Convex — bends up in big moves.
w_duration Catch the flight-to-quality. Treasuries that rally when scared money flees stocks. Convex (conditionally) — the deflation hedge.

So the bend in Figure 1 isn’t magic — it’s two of the three terms. The equity term is the straight line; w_convexity + w_duration are the hedges that bend it upward in the tail.

The canonical split (the model’s a-priori default)

equity 0.65
cvx 0.15
duration 0.20

Notice w_duration is the plug: the model sets w_equity and w_convexity, and duration is simply 1 − w_equity − w_convexity — whatever budget is left after participation and crisis-alpha. That’s why the equation is written the way it is: it’s a constraint, not three free choices.

Two dials sit on top of this static split:
① The glidepath moves w_equity for your life-stage — 0.65 (balanced) up to 0.95 (accumulation, the accumulation-phase setting) and back down toward 0.65 as you near drawdown years.
② The volatility brake scales the equity exposure down automatically as markets get turbulent: eq_scale = min(1, vol_target ÷ realized_vol) with vol_target = 12%/yr.
Figure 3. The brake. While realized volatility is at or below the 12% target the portfolio holds full equity; once volatility spikes, equity is scaled back proportionally — in days, with no forecast required. (A confirmatory hard cap of 0.70 applies in liquidity-stress regimes.)

3 · How the specific ETFs fit each term

Each term is filled by instruments chosen for the shape they contribute — not for a return forecast.

equity 0.65 SPY core (60% of the sleeve) + up to 5 IC-scored factor/sector tilts (40%) — currently VLUE · XLK · MTUM · PDBC · USO, with a guaranteed value slot. SPY is the straight line — broad market participation. The tilts lean toward factors the panel measures as currently rewarded; a de-dup guard stops near-twins (e.g. it keeps no-K-1 PDBC over DBC).
convexity 0.15 DBMF (50%) · KMLM (30%) · BTAL (20%). Before these funds existed, the model uses a point-in-time proxy basket TLT / GLD / DBC (what trend-followers actually hold). DBMF & KMLM are managed-futures / trend-following — they go short assets that are falling, so a sustained crash is their best environment. That is a literally convex payoff: a small bleed in calm chop, a big positive when a trend (up or down) persists. BTAL is long-low-beta / short-high-beta — it rises when the market falls.

The model lengthens duration (TLT) when that hedge is working and switches to TIPS (VTIP) when inflation would make nominal bonds fail (2022).
Figure 4. The convexity sleeve on its own. Trend-following has a smile: it pays in big sustained moves (its best case is a crash) and costs a small premium in flat, directionless markets — the textbook shape of positive convexity, like owning a straddle.

4 · Why two hedges, not one

The single most important design decision: no one hedge works in every crisis. Bonds are a brilliant hedge — until inflation, when they crash with stocks (2022). Trend-following is a brilliant hedge — but needs a sustained move to work. Hold both, and at least one is firing in almost every kind of selloff.

Crisis typeEquity Convexity (trend) Duration (bonds)

Read it across each row: there’s always a green cell. 2022 is the proof — bonds failed, but the trend sleeve carried the protection. A bonds-only “60/40” had no green that year; Convex Core did.

6 · The regime engine — reading the market’s weather

You’ve seen two parts of the model move on their own: the duration sleeve changes flavor (Section 3) and equity gets capped in a crisis. A small, transparent classifier decides which “weather” we’re in and drives those changes. It emits one primary state plus independent tags — all computed point-in-time from local data, no forecasts.

① It picks one state, by precedence

Five mutually-exclusive states are checked top to bottom. The first one whose conditions all hold wins — so stress always wins, and “neutral” is the fallback when the signals disagree.

LIQUIDITY_STRESS — VIX jumping into its top 20% + high-yield cracking vs quality bonds + SPY under its 50/200-day averages  →  cap equity at ≤0.70× & most-defensive bonds
RISK_OFF — SPY below its 200-day, 50-day below 200-day (downtrend), VIX in its top 30%, credit deteriorating  →  lengthen duration (TLT)
RECOVERY — recently fell below the averages but is reclaiming them, VIX falling from an elevated level  →  begin re-risking
RISK_ON — SPY above a rising 200-day, positive 63-day return, VIX below its median, breadth >50% above the 200-day  →  full equity, neutral bonds
RISK_NEUTRAL (residual) — mixed signals  →  the default; neutral bonds
Figure 6. The precedence ladder. Checked top-down, first match wins. The order encodes a bias: it’s quick to get defensive (stress & risk-off sit at the top) and slower to re-risk (recovery must prove itself).

② What it reads — four families of signals

Every condition is just a fact about today’s tape, seen through four lenses:

LensThe actual inputsWhat it tells you
🌡️ Volatility / fearVIX 20-day change, its 252-day percentile, VIX vs its own median Is fear spiking — and how extreme is it versus the last year?
💳 CreditHYG vs LQD relative strength (20- & 63-day) Junk bonds underperforming quality = credit stress building before stocks crack
📈 Trend / structureSPY vs its 50- & 200-day moving averages; 63-day return Is price above or below trend, and rising or falling?
📊 Breadth% of stocks above their own 200-day MA Is the move broad, or a handful of names carrying a thin market?
Graceful degradation. If a signal is temporarily missing, its clause just drops — but a state still needs ≥3 live clauses to qualify, so a blind input can never silently flip the regime.

③ It refuses to flap — a 3-day confirmation

A new state only takes effect after its conditions hold three sessions in a row. A one-day scare can’t whipsaw the whole book.

Five orthogonal overlay tags flag specific weather independently of the primary state — any can be on at once:

e.g. INFLATION_STRESS fires when energy/materials lead, long bonds are weak, and breakevens are rising — exactly the signal that flips the bond hedge to TIPS.

Honest caveat — the regime layer is a refinement, not the engine. In walk-forward testing, turning it fully off moves Sortino only 1.08 → 1.06. The volatility brake (Figure 3) — which reacts in days with no classification lag — does most of the de-risking; the regime layer’s job is the finer one: pick the right kind of bond hedge and add the liquidity-stress cap. It’s kept because it’s cheap, transparent and robust — not because it carries the result.

7 · So how did the equation come to be?

It’s the shortest expression of four beliefs, each backed by published evidence:

  1. Own the market. Stocks drift up over time, so the core must always participate → a w_equity term that never exits.
  2. Drawdowns, not volatility, destroy compounding. A −50% hole needs +100% back (Figure 2) → spend part of the budget on crash insurance, not on chasing a bit more return.
  3. No single hedge is enough (Section 4) → split the insurance into two orthogonal convex sleeves: trend-following (w_convexity) and Treasuries (w_duration).
  4. Don’t overfit. The weights are frozen a-priori (0.65 / 0.15 / 0.20), not optimized to the past; only the glidepath dial and the mechanical vol-brake move. Because the three must fully invest one dollar, they sum to 1, and duration takes the residual.

Put together: w_equity gives you the upside line; w_convexity + w_duration bend it up in the tail; the brake trims equity when storms hit. The result is the asymmetric, convex payoff of Figure 1 — keep most of the upside, lose far less of the downside.

One-sentence summary: Convex Core owns the market with most of its money, then spends the rest on two different kinds of crash insurance that each pay off in a different kind of crisis — so the portfolio’s return curve bends in your favor exactly when it matters.

8 · An open question — could the equity core be different?

The equity sleeve is SPY plus a measured tilt overlay (Section 3). A natural question: should the core be something else — total-market VTI, international VXUS, small-cap IWM — and should it lean by valuation / CAPE when the U.S. looks expensive? It’s worth being precise here, because the model already has an opinion.

CandidateWhat it isValuation leanHow Convex Core already reaches it
SPYS&P 500 — large-cap U.S. (today’s base)The expensive end (elevated CAPE) It is the core — the “straight line” of Figure 1.
VTITotal U.S. market (adds mid/small)≈ SPY (large-caps dominate the cap-weight) Negligible difference — historically tracks SPY within noise.
VXUSTotal international ex-U.S.Cheaper (lower CAPE), currency + diversification The intl_equity tilt sleeve — added when its score earns a slot, not as a fixed bet.
IWMRussell 2000 small-capsCheaper / value-ish, higher volatility Factor exposure comes through the scored tilts (e.g. MTUM, VLUE) — more surgical than a blanket small-cap bet.
What CAPE is. The Shiller CAPE divides price by the last 10 years of real earnings — a valuation gauge. A high CAPE has historically meant lower 10-year forward returns. The U.S. (SPY) has sat at an elevated CAPE for years while international, value and small-cap have looked cheaper — which is the whole case for a valuation tilt.

So why is the core still SPY? Two honest reasons, both from this project’s own research:

① We tested CAPE timing — it didn’t earn its keep. A full CAPE-driven allocation (best config: a rolling-30-year baseline + a trend tilt) reached a Sharpe of about 0.78 — well short of Convex Core’s 1.32. CAPE tells you about long-run expected return, not when to act; as a timing signal it’s weak and slow (decade horizon, enormous noise).

② “Cheap” stayed cheap. “Tilt to the cheapest market” has been a losing trade for ~15 years — the U.S. has been the expensive one the whole time and kept winning. And a separate universe-refresh experiment found that swapping in more/younger funds mostly adds overfitting surface, not edge (verdict: don’t modernize).
The model’s answer is already a valuation tilt — just a measured one. Instead of hard-coding VXUS or IWM, the IC-scored overlay leans toward international, value or small-cap exactly when the data rewards it, with a guaranteed value slot and a de-dup guard. A static CAPE/valuation overlay is a fair candidate for a pre-registered, gated experiment — it would have to beat the current tilt net of costs and survive walk-forward before it ships — not a default change. That’s the same parameter-minimal, prove-it-or-publish-the-negative-result discipline behind every other knob in the model. (This applies identically to Convex Prime, which runs the same equity sleeve, levered.)
