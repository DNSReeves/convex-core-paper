Summary. Treating the model's two continuous dials (w_equity × vol_target, 28 configurations) as the trial set over a 245-month panel (2006-02-28 → 2026-06-30), the deployed configuration's Sharpe survives multiple-testing deflation overwhelmingly: Deflated Sharpe Ratio = 0.99998 (minimum track record ≈ 40 months vs. 245 available). The configurations are near-identical (annualized-Sharpe coefficient of variation 2.5%), and the deployed configuration ranks 27ᵗʰ of 28 on Sharpe — it was chosen by a-priori risk posture, not by maximizing the backtest, so selection bias cannot have inflated it. The CSCV Probability of Backtest Overfitting = 0.54 reflects that the dials are not meaningfully distinguishable in-sample (selecting a "best" dial is sampling noise), while every selected configuration remains profitable out-of-sample in 100% of 12,870 splits. The diagnostics corroborate the paper's parameter-minimal, deterministic design rather than revealing a tuned edge.

A1 · Why this test

A backtest's Sharpe ratio can be inflated two ways that have nothing to do with genuine edge: by selection (trying many configurations and reporting the luckiest) and by non-normality (fat tails and skew that make a given Sharpe less informative than the Gaussian assumption implies). The paper's defense is structural — the model is deterministic, its parameters fixed a-priori, and its negative results published — but the review board asked for the quantitative complement: the Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR), which discounts the observed Sharpe for both the number of trials and the return distribution's shape, and the Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO), which measures whether choosing the in-sample-best configuration generalizes out-of-sample.

A2 · Method

Trial set

The honest count of "trials" is the model's genuine search space. Convex Core exposes two continuous dials — the equity-weight target w_equity and the volatility target vol_target. We sweep w_equity ∈ {0.65, 0.70, 0.75, 0.80, 0.85, 0.90, 0.95} × vol_target ∈ {0.10, 0.12, 0.14, 0.16} = 28 configurations, each run through the same deterministic engine (same regime layer, sleeves, brake, and costs) and reduced to a monthly total-return stream. The deployed paper configuration is (w_equity=0.95, vol_target=0.12). All 28 streams share a common 245-month window.

Deflated Sharpe Ratio

The DSR is the probability that the deployed configuration's true Sharpe exceeds the expected maximum Sharpe under the null that no configuration has skill — where that expected maximum grows with the number of trials and the dispersion of Sharpe across them — evaluated through the Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio, which corrects for the sample's skewness and kurtosis and length. DSR > 0.95 means the result survives the multiple-testing penalty. (Bailey & López de Prado, 2014.)

Probability of Backtest Overfitting (CSCV)

Combinatorially-Symmetric Cross-Validation splits the 245-month panel into 16 contiguous blocks of 15 months. For every one of the 12,870 symmetric ways to assign half the blocks in-sample and half out-of-sample, it selects the configuration with the best in-sample Sharpe and records that configuration's out-of-sample rank. PBO is the fraction of splits in which the in-sample winner lands below the out-of-sample median. (Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado & Zhu, 2017.)

A3 · Result — parameter insensitivity and the Deflated Sharpe Ratio

Across the entire 28-configuration grid the annualized Sharpe spans only 1.024–1.115 (mean 1.075, standard deviation 0.027, coefficient of variation 2.5%). The model is strikingly insensitive to its dials: there is no poor configuration to avoid and no standout configuration to cherry-pick. Tellingly, the deployed configuration ranks 27ᵗʰ of 28 — near the bottom on Sharpe — because w_equity=0.95 was selected for accumulation-phase upside capture (an a-priori risk-posture choice), not to flatter the backtest.

PBO = 0.54 reads, against a naïve <0.5 rule of thumb, as a borderline figure — but the correct reading is that it is the expected null here. CSCV asks whether the in-sample-best configuration generalizes; when the configurations are near-identical (Sharpe CoV 2.5%, Table 1), there is no genuinely-best configuration to identify, so the in-sample winner is chosen by sampling noise and lands near the out-of-sample median by construction (median OOS rank 0.46). The diagnostic that actually carries signal is the out-of-sample outcome of the selected configuration: it is profitable in 100% of the 12,870 splits (P(OOS Sharpe < 0) = 0.000). The mildly negative degradation slope (-0.96) is precisely why the model fixes its dials a-priori instead of optimizing them — chasing the highest in-sample Sharpe hands a little back out-of-sample. None of this is overfitting in the sense PBO was designed to expose (a tuned strategy that collapses out-of-sample); it is the fingerprint of a parameter-insensitive design.

A5 · Interpretation — what this does and does not establish

It establishes: (1) the deployed Sharpe is not a multiple-testing illusion — it survives deflation with a 40-month minimum track record against 245 months of data; (2) the model is parameter-insensitive over its dial space (2.5% Sharpe CoV), so there is no configuration-selection edge to overfit; (3) the deployment choice was not Sharpe-maximizing (rank 27/28), foreclosing the cherry-picking failure mode by construction; (4) across 12,870 out-of-sample folds the family never loses money.

It does not establish live performance, edge over the strongest balanced benchmark on risk-adjusted ratios (the paper's §8 is explicit that the advantage over 40/60 is in return, not risk), or robustness to structural choices outside the two dials swept here (sleeve definitions, regime cutoffs, universe) — those are probed by the §13 ablation grid and §6 walk-forward, not by this test. CSCV's PBO is also least informative exactly when trials are near-identical, as they are here; we therefore lean on the accompanying out-of-sample loss probability and the non-optimized deployment rank rather than the PBO point estimate alone. These results sharpen, and are consistent with, the paper's §3.1 (determinism, no fitted parameters) and §15 (limitations).
